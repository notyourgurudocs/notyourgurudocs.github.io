What "Other Duties as Assigned" Really Means

Every under-resourced workplace runs on the same small set of soft, plausible-sounding phrases. They sound reasonable in the moment — that's exactly why they work. Here are five of the most common ones, translated.

"Other duties as assigned."

Translation: There's no ceiling on what can be asked for this salary. It's usually one line in your offer letter, agreed to before you ever saw the job in practice — and it's doing more work than any other sentence in the document.

"We're like a family here."

Translation: Families don't have performance reviews. This phrase almost never shows up during a raise conversation — it shows up right before an unpaid ask, doing the work of making that ask feel like an honor instead of a request.

"Let's circle back on comp after this project."

Translation: The project ends. The conversation often doesn't, unless you're the one who brings it back up — in writing, with a date attached.

"Everyone's wearing a lot of hats right now."

Translation: The company chose not to hire enough people, and "everyone" is doing the work of making that choice feel shared instead of personal.

"You're so dependable, I don't even have to ask."

Translation: Your own consistency is being used to skip a conversation that should be happening. Trust, here, is functioning as leverage — not as a compliment.

Once you see the pattern, the individual phrases stop working on you one at a time.

None of this means every version of these sentences is a red flag in every context — but when the same shape keeps showing up, in different words, across months, it stops being an explanation and starts being an answer.
