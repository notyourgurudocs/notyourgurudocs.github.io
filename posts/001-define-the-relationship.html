Why "So What Are We?" Never Gets You a Real Answer

You've asked it. Probably more than once. Probably late at night, probably after you'd already rehearsed it in your head for a week. And the answer — if you got one at all — was some version of "I don't know, I like spending time with you." Which is not an answer. It's a delay dressed up as honesty.

Here's why that specific question keeps failing you, and what actually works instead.

It's an open-ended question about a decision someone doesn't want to make

"So what are we?" hands the other person an easy out, because it's phrased as a request for information, not a decision point. It's genuinely easy to answer "I don't know" to a question about what something is. It's much harder to say "I don't know" to a direct proposal.

It happens in the moment, which favors whoever wants to avoid it

Ambush conversations — the ones that start with "can we talk?" in bed at midnight — favor the person who's comfortable staying vague under pressure. If someone has spent months avoiding a label, a surprise conversation doesn't suddenly make them ready to commit. It just makes them better at deflecting on the spot.

What works instead: a statement, not a question

The fix isn't a better version of "what are we." It's dropping the question format entirely. Something like: "I want to be exclusive and call this what it is. If you're there too, let's talk. If you're not, I need to know that too." That's not a question anyone can shrug off with "I don't know." It's a position, and it asks for a real response to a real position.

You're not asking for permission to want clarity. You're stating that you do.

The answer you get will still tell you everything

A direct statement gets one of three responses: agreement, an honest "I'm not there," or a vague dodge. All three are useful. The first two give you real information. The third — more deflection after a direct statement — is itself the clearest answer of all.
