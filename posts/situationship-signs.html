5 Signs You're in a Situationship, Not a Relationship

You've been "talking" for four months. You've met his roommate but not his friends. Every time you bring up what this is, the conversation gets vague and you end up apologizing for asking. If any of that sounds familiar, you're not in a slow-burn relationship. You're in a situationship — and the sooner you name it, the sooner you can decide what to do about it.

Here are five signs that separate a situationship from an actual relationship, no sugarcoating.

1. Plans never go further than "this weekend"

Real relationships have a future tense. Situationships live entirely in the present — "let's see" is the default answer to anything more than a few days out. If you can't picture a trip, a holiday, or even next month with this person because it's never been discussed, that's not spontaneity. That's an exit ramp he's keeping open.

2. You've never met the people who matter to him

Access to someone's actual life — their close friends, their family, their coworkers — is not a small thing. It's a decision. If months have passed and you're still a stranger to everyone who matters to him, you haven't been introduced because you haven't been decided on.

3. "What are we?" always gets deflected

Watch what happens the moment you ask a direct question. A healthy answer sounds like an answer. A situationship answer sounds like a mood: "I don't know, why does it have to be a thing?" That's not confusion. That's someone avoiding a conversation that might end with them having to commit or lose you.

4. The good moments outweigh the pattern, not the other way around

Every situationship has good days — that's exactly what keeps you in it. The question isn't whether the good moments are real. It's whether they're the exception to the pattern or the pattern itself. If you're mentally collecting evidence for the defense every time someone asks how it's going, you already know the verdict.

5. You're doing the emotional labor of a relationship without the label

You check in when he's had a bad day. You remember what he told you last week. You've adjusted your plans around his. Meanwhile, the relationship itself has no name, no exclusivity, no direction. You're already doing the work — you're just doing it for free.

Clarity is not the risk here. Staying in the guessing game is.

None of this means the person is a villain. It usually just means the ambiguity is working for him and costing you. The fix isn't more patience — it's a direct conversation, on your terms, with a script that doesn't leave room for another "let's see."
